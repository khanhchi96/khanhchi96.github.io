The oil price crisis in 2017 - 2018

Crisis scene

Bowler (2015) reported that "the initial dramatic drop was from $110 a barrel of Brent oil in June 2014 to $80 a barrel before the OPEC meeting in late November". Thereafter, the Brent oil price fell significantly to below £50 a barrel by early January 2015, before experiencing a slight recovery to about £65 a barrel in May 2015. In contrast, medium-term Brent futures did not receive a material drop below the established $90–$100 a barrel range. At least, not until after the OPEC meeting, when they immediately adjusted to about £70–£75 a barrel and continued to kept this new range since the first half of December, even when the substantial rally in spot prices was taken place since the start of the year.
Higher supply projections stemmed from positive non-OPEC developments (especially US shale oil) and the beyond-expectation OPEC output in Iraq, Libya, and Saudi Arabia while the below-expectation demand stemmed mostly from Europe and Asia.
Following the decision made by OPEC in late November which was not to cut down oil production, prices fell quickly by about 20% as markets fundamentally changed expectations about future OPEC supply. Medium-term oil futures also adjusted sharply. (Kottasova, 2014).

OPEC's nations

In OPEC’s November 27th meeting, there was a heated debate among OPEC members about "the best way to respond to the falling oil prices" but in the end, OPEC could not quite come to an agreement and ended up keeping the same amount of production. On behalf of the members, OPEC Secretary-General Abdalla El-Badri confirmed that they would produce 30 million barrels a day in the following 6 months, while continuously watching how the market behaves. (Plumer, 2015)
That undoubtedly caused the oil price to crash even further. The price of Brent crude went from £80 per barrel to £70 per barrel in just a few days. And it kept tumbling to down below £60 per barrel by mid-December and £50 by January. Plumer (2015) stated that for all intents and purposes, OPEC was in a "price war" with the US. What that meant was that while it was relatively cheap to pump oil out of places like Saudi Arabia and Kuwait, it was however, more expensive to extract oil from Texas and North Dakota or other shale formations.
